Feature Set
1. Conversation & Companionship
This is the soul of the product. The device is always available to just talk. No task required.

Open-ended conversation in their native language — Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, Marathi, Gujarati, Kannada, Punjabi, Odia
Remembers everything said across all conversations — names, preferences, health concerns, family details, past stories shared
Asks follow-up questions based on memory ("Last time you mentioned your son was visiting — did he come?")
Recognizes emotional tone and adapts — if someone sounds lonely or low, it shifts toward comfort and warmth rather than information
Never ends a conversation abruptly — always closes warmly


2. Religious & Spiritual Knowledge
This is your deepest content moat.
Storytelling

Stories from all 18 Mahapuranas, Ramayana (all regional versions — Valmiki, Tulsidas, Kamban, Adhyatma), Mahabharata, Bhagavata
Panchatantra, Jataka tales, Hitopadesha
Stories of saints — Mirabai, Tukaram, Kabir, Andal, Ramakrishna, Vivekananda, Ramanuja, Adi Shankaracharya, Guru Nanak, Mahavir
Regional folk versions of myths — the Ramayana in Kerala is different from the one in Bengal, and elderly users will know this and appreciate that the device knows it too
Ability to tell the same story in different depths — a 2-minute version or a 20-minute version depending on mood

Daily Spiritual Content

Shloka or verse of the day with meaning explained in simple language
Daily prayer assistant — it can lead them through their morning or evening prayer sequence
Festival calendar — automatically knows when Ekadashi is, when Navratri starts, what each day of a festival signifies
Significance of the day — "Today is Margashirsha Purnima, which is considered especially auspicious for this reason..."

Q&A on Texts

They can ask anything — "Why did Krishna say this to Arjuna?", "What is the difference between Dvaita and Advaita?", "What does the Gita say about death?"
Answers calibrated for a non-scholarly elderly person — no jargon, always relatable

Multi-faith

Covers Hinduism, Sikhism, Jainism, and Islam at depth — India's elderly are not monolithic


3. Music & Audio
You need this to compete with the music box, but you go deeper.

Bhajan library — by deity, by saint, by language, by occasion
Classical ragas by time of day — device suggests the right raga for morning, afternoon, evening, night
Explains why a raga fits a time — this turns passive listening into learning
Devotional music for specific festivals and occasions
Can sing along or hum familiar tunes to prompt memory in dementia-adjacent cases
Guided meditation music rooted in Indian tradition — not generic wellness sounds


4. Memory & Notes

Voice-recorded personal notes — "Remember that my daughter's anniversary is March 12th"
The device proactively surfaces these at the right time — "You asked me to remind you about your daughter's anniversary, it's in three days"
Records important family information — grandchildren's names, their schools, their birthdays
Can store prayers or personal verses they want to remember
Life story recording — the device prompts them with questions and records their answers as an ongoing audio memoir


5. Reminders & Daily Routine

Medication reminders — multiple times a day, persistent until acknowledged
Prayer time reminders aligned to their specific tradition
Water and meal reminders — gentle, non-patronizing
Doctor appointment reminders
Festival and puja day reminders set weeks in advance
Morning routine prompt — "Good morning. Today is Tuesday, Hanuman's day. Your blood pressure medicine reminder is at 9am. Shall I tell you today's shloka?"


6. Family Connection
This is a major stickiness and monetization lever.

Family members install a companion app on their phone
They can record voice messages that play on the device at a set time — a grandchild's "Good morning Dadi" playing every morning is worth more than any feature
Grandparent can reply by voice, message goes back to the family app
Family can send photos that display if you add a small screen, or the device describes them verbally
Family can see device activity — not surveillance, but a gentle "Nani spoke to the device for 45 minutes today" that gives remote family peace of mind
SOS button — one physical press calls a pre-set family member immediately


7. Health & Wellness
Positioned within their cultural framework, not as a medical product.

Guided pranayama — Anulom Vilom, Bhramari, Kapalbhati — explained in traditional terms
Yoga nidra for sleep — deeply rooted in Indian tradition, not Western wellness
Gentle movement prompts — framed as what their tradition recommends, not exercise
Mood check-ins — casual, conversational, never clinical
Identifies concerning patterns over time and can notify family if someone hasn't spoken to the device in an unusual amount of time


8. General Knowledge & Curiosity
Elderly people are not intellectually dead. Many are deeply curious.

Current affairs explained simply — what's happening in the country, explained without political bias
History — India's independence movement, Partition stories, stories about their era that they personally lived through
Geography, science explained through Indian context — "Do you know why the Ganges doesn't stagnate the way other rivers do?"
Trivia and quizzes rooted in Indian culture and mythology — this creates engagement and mental stimulation


9. Mental Stimulation & Games
Fighting cognitive decline is a real need.

Memory games — the device tells a short story and asks questions about it
Name-that-deity games — clues about attributes and the user guesses
Shayari and poetry games — the device says a line and the user completes it
Simple riddles rooted in Indian folk tradition
